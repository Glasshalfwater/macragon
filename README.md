# Macragon

### Hexagonal macropad powered by a Waveshare 2040-zero with multiple keyboard layers, USB-C connectivity, RGB LEDs, and planned voice keyboard functionality.

## Origins:
To be honest, my main motivation behind this project is mostly laziness :>. Long story short, my engineering class's keyboards are a bit annoying. To use them properly, one has to actually sit in their chair very close to one's respective desk (it's more tiring than it sounds after an hour and a half). The chair arms are too far apart to support the keyboard away from the desk, and the rubber pads on the bottom of the keyboard mean that dust and debris will collect on your pants if the keyboard is in your lap. So, what I wanted to do is to have a little mic I could carry around that functioned as a voice keyboard, so I could actually slouch in my chair comfortably at a distance. That, combined with me seeing a lot of other Hackclub projects being macropads, meant I decided to make a fusion between the two. Ideally, the goal was to learn how to read and make schematics, PCB design, and improve my coding skills. The (farfetched) dream would be to have a voice keyboard that could recognize CAD commands while also having buttons for manual functions. Unfortunately, implementing a voice keyboard that plugs in as a USB audio device was harder than I expected in Python (I had decided to use KMK instead of QMK), compounded by my lack of experience in Python and with USB audio communication. As such, it has been left off until v2 of the macragon. However, the macropad keys still work, and as a bonus they have RGB LEDs. Additionally, the hardware for the microphone is on the PCB, and QMK supports the microprocessor, etc., so adding a voice keyboard will simply be a firmware update.

Another segment of motivation is just my fingers getting mad at me after I'd been creating hexagons and other repetitive tasks in Onshape without convienient keybinds for two hours a day for a week. (Yeah, I probably should have just unbound some other keybind I wasn't using, but macropads are cooler.)

On the other hand, choosing the shape of my macropad was [easy](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thOifuHs6eY).

## Setup 
In the event you are building this yourself, the [necessary materials](/BOM.csv), the required files to [order and assemble the PCB](/hardware), and the files to [3D print the case](/hardware) are can be found above. The locations for where to solder components can be viewed by opening the .epro file for the PCB, and a [guide on soldering SMD components](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GAjuZoyAhgI&themeRefresh=1) is linked in the credits. After you have acquired assembled the hardware necessary, plug the Waveshare 2040-zero into your computer via USB-C data cable, and follow the instructions on the [Waveshare Wiki](https://www.waveshare.com/wiki/RP2040-Zero) to place the board in bootloader mode. Afterwards, the board should show up in your File Explorer (or equivalent) as a removable USB device, upon which you should to place the [.uf2 CircuitPython firmware file](firmware\adafruit-circuitpython-waveshare_rp2040_zero-en_US-10.2.1.uf2) into the USB drive. 

In order to install [KMK](https://github.com/KMKfw/kmk_firmware/blob/main/docs/en/Getting_Started.md) (circuitpython fork and keyboard firmware) onto the board, [download the latest version of KMK](https://github.com/KMKfw/kmk_firmware/archive/refs/heads/main.zip) from the main branch. Then, extract the kmk_firmware-main folder and copy the kmk folder within on to the Waveshare board while the board is in bootloader mode (will require a new bootloader session), where it should show up as a CIRCUITPY drive. Replace the board.py/code.py file with the [main.py](firmware\src\main.py) file located in /firmware/src, which contains the keymap and two layers that I have created. Of course, if you want to make your own key layers, animations, and macros/, etc., just edit the main.py file (links to KMK docs on a [list of keycodes](https://github.com/KMKfw/kmk_firmware/blob/main/docs/en/keycodes.md), [layers](https://github.com/KMKfw/kmk_firmware/blob/main/docs/en/layers.md), [RGB LED io](https://github.com/KMKfw/kmk_firmware/blob/main/docs/en/rgb.md) and [macros](https://github.com/KMKfw/kmk_firmware/blob/main/docs/en/layers.md)). In addition, you will need to download and then place the appropriate version of the [board module](https://circuitpython.org/libraries) into the CIRCUITPY drive. After that, take the board out of bootloader and you should be good to go!

Google is your friend (just don't read the EULA or privacy policy too closely), so if something unexpected happens, look up information! There is almost certainly info and tutorials online about how to fix it and those who have experienced it before (unless you happen to be one Newton Pulsifer, in which case just try to break it I guess?). In addition, the documentation for setting up various aspects of the firmware is linked in the above paragraphs and at the bottom of this README.
